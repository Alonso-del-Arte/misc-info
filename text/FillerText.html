Filler Text

Filler text. This is filler text. It does not com­mu­ni­cate anything par­tic­u­lar­ly im­por­tant or insightful, but it should at least be gram­mat­i­cal­ly cor­rect. I be­lieve that filler text should be in the same lan­guage as the larger text where it is to function as a placeholder.

Another feature of this filler text is that we have made an effort to include each letter of the English alphabet at least twice: at least once as an uppercase letter at the beginning of a sentence, and at least once as a lowercase letter. Obviously, each letter occurs more frequently in this text as a lowercase letter than as an uppercase letter.

Generally, filler text should not con­tain statements that are prov­ably true or provably false. That can be ac­com­plished through vague­ness, by with­hold­ing the necessary context to ascertain the ve­rac­i­ty of a given state­ment.

Or, alternatively, filler text can con­tain statements that are prov­ably true, but trivially so. Such as, for example, that true is the op­po­site of false, and false is the opposite of true.
